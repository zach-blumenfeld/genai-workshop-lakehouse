HYPOTHETICAL (not used today -- needs deeper docs): the "full job quote"
================================================================================

PROMPT (outline-DEPTH driven):
  "Quote the complete timing job on a Falcon 2.0T -- every procedure and
   sub-procedure involved, with the parts and total labor hours."

WHY IT'S PARKED: our manuals are shallow (2-3 levels; a job chains only ~3-4
linked procedures that share vocabulary), so a similarity arm can rebuild it with
a couple of searches. Not enough structure to miss -> no clean failure. (Same
"too-shallow" trap as the single-car misfire.)

HOW IT WOULD WORK WITH REALISTIC DOCS: real OEM service manuals nest deeply and
cross-reference heavily -- a major job (timing belt on an interference engine)
fans out into 10-20 sub-procedures across the manual: drain coolant, remove
accessory drive belt, support the engine / drop the mount, remove covers, lock
cam/crank, replace tensioner + idlers + water pump, reseal, refill, VVT relearn --
each its own section with its own parts and labor line, reached only by following
"remove X to access Y" cross-references.

At that depth the shapes separate cleanly:
  - outline (DEPTH) -> traverse the full procedure tree and assemble EVERY
    sub-step in order. Similarity SAMPLES the obvious "timing" chunks and misses
    the linked sub-procedures (different vocabulary: "engine mount", "accessory
    belt") -> the quote UNDERCOUNTS parts + labor, and the agent can't know what
    cross-references it didn't follow.
  - connections -> join each PROC-* node to warehouse labor_hours + part numbers
    (incl. supersession) and total it.
  - failure = a materially WRONG estimate (missing sub-steps) vs a complete,
    auditable one. Visceral and measurable.

To enable later: deepen the manual outline (nested sub-procedures + explicit
"remove X to access Y" LINKS_TO chains, 8+ deep) for a few flagship jobs. Then the
depth-driven outline failure becomes as clean as the breadth-driven coverage one.

CONNECTIONS-SHAPE FAILURE SCENARIO (text-to-SQL on large / ambiguous schemas)
General -- NOT an AutoFix result (our 6-table schema is too small to show it; this
failure only appears at enterprise scale).
================================================================================

THE SETTING: a real warehouse has hundreds of tables, many near-identical, often
very wide (hundreds of columns each). The full schema is far larger than a prompt
can hold.

WITHOUT THE SHAPE (text-to-SQL over the raw schema):
  - The model can't fit the schema, so it brute-forces metadata at query time:
    list tables -> inspect columns -> guess -> retry. On ambiguous, look-alike
    tables it picks the wrong one, loops, and on cheaper models often gives up
    with no answer after many calls.
  - Even when it eventually lands the right table, it drags bulky schema metadata
    (mostly empty/null column fields) into context and re-sends it every turn ->
    high, repeated token cost.
  - Net: unreliable schema-linking AND expensive.

WITH THE SHAPE (a semantic layer / graph over the schema -- "connections"):
  - Retrieves the RIGHT table + columns by MEANING before generating SQL (semantic
    search over the schema graph), so the model never has to scan the haystack.
  - Holds the join paths explicitly (foreign keys as edges) -- multi-hop joins are
    given, not guessed.
  - Returns a COMPACT, relevant slice (table + the needed columns/types), not the
    whole schema or a bulky metadata envelope re-sent each turn.
  - Net: cheap models become reliable (right table on the first pass) AND much
    cheaper (no brute-force crawl, no schema re-sent every turn).

THE POINT (connections shape): the warehouse schema is itself a structure --
tables, columns, FK relationships as a navigable graph. Retrieve the relevant
sub-structure instead of dumping the whole schema or letting the model guess. That
is what lets text-to-SQL scale to large, ambiguous schemas where a raw-schema
approach is both unreliable and costly.
